# Diegetic Design — Loose Suggestions

Ideas for replacing abstract game systems with in-world equivalents. Nothing here is committed to — just possibilities to pull from when the time is right.

---

## Boundaries

- **Tree lines, cliffs, rivers, ocean** — natural borders. A dense forest the player can't walk through is a wall that tells a story.
- **Guarded gates** — a road leading out of town with a guard who says "too dangerous beyond here" gives a soft lock that can become a quest unlock later.
- **Broken bridges, flooded paths** — seasonal or quest-gated boundaries that feel like the world has reasons, not rules.

## Feedback & Information

- **Town crier / notice board** — instead of tutorial popups, a board in the town square with pinned notices ("Wheat prices soaring in the east!"). The ledger already fits this philosophy.
- **Merchant gossip** — NPCs casually mentioning price trends or events replaces a market ticker. Oblivion did this with overheard rumors.
- **Church bells / rooster crow** — time-of-day feedback through sound rather than a clock HUD.
- **Worn paths between buildings** — visual storytelling that subtly guides the player toward points of interest without waypoints.

## Economy & Progression

- **Cart / pack animal** — inventory limits visualized by how loaded the horse looks, not a number in a corner.
- **Shop displays** — what an artisan has in stock visible on their market stall before opening the trade menu.
- **Building condition** — a town thriving or declining based on trade activity, reflected in the buildings themselves.

## Navigation

- **Signposts at crossroads** — pointing toward towns with distance, replacing a minimap.
- **Landmarks visible from a distance** — a church steeple, a windmill, smoke from a smithy. The player orients by the world, not a compass.
- **Lanterns and torchlight at night** — draw the player toward safe areas organically.

---

**The common thread:** if you're about to add a UI element or invisible system, ask *"what would this be in a medieval town?"* The answer is almost always more interesting than the abstraction.
